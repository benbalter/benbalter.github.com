For the past decade+, I've tracked every reorg I've been through - 25 reorgs and 13 managers, averaging a reorg every six months and a new manager each year. Reorgs are a disruptive fact of life in tech, and they can be a major source of stress and uncertainty, but they don't have to be.

Why reorgs happen — They're rarely malicious or even strategic. Orgs reorg because priorities shift, leaders change, headcount fluctuates, and Conway's Law keeps asserting itself. Most reorgs aren't about you.

Why they feel so disruptive — Loss of context and relationships, renegotiating trust with a new manager, uncertainty about priorities. The disruption is real—but predictable.

The mindset shift: treat reorgs as a constant, not an exception — If you've been in tech for any length of time, a reorg every 6 months is baseline. Design your career and work habits for that reality: portable relationships, documented decisions, work that speaks for itself.

The long view — Your manager changes, your team changes, your org chart changes. What persists is your reputation, your relationships, and the actual work. Optimize for those.
